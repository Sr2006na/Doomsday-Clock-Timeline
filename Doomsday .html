History of the Doomsday Clock

The Doomsday Clock was created in 1947 by artist Martyl Langsdorf and the Bulletin of the Atomic Scientists, founded by Albert Einstein and University of Chicago scientists who helped develop the first atomic weapons. Originally conceived as a cover design for the Bulletin's magazine, it evolved into a powerful metaphor for humanity's vulnerability to existential threats.

"The Clock represents the judgment of leading scientists and security experts about the likelihood of global catastrophe."
- Bulletin of the Atomic Scientists

Initially focused on nuclear danger, the Clock's scope expanded in 2007 to include climate change. In 2020, it began measuring in seconds rather than minutes for the first time, reflecting increased urgency. The Clock is set annually by the Bulletin's Science and Security Board in consultation with its Board of Sponsors, which includes 13 Nobel Laureates.

Key Historical Facts

  • Created by Manhattan Project scientists after WWII
  • First appeared on Bulletin's June 1947 cover
  • Original setting: 7 minutes to midnight
  • 23 time changes in 76+ year history
  • Furthest from midnight: 17 minutes (1991)
  • Closest to midnight: 89 seconds (2025-present)
1947
7 minutes to midnight

Clock debut as journal cover design symbolizing nuclear danger

1949
3 minutes

The Soviet Union tests a nuclear bomb similar to the U.S.'s, igniting the arms race.

1953
2 minutes

The U.S. develops and tests the powerful hydrogen bomb in 1952, soon followed by the Soviet Union.

1960
7 minutes

Despite tensions, the U.S. and USSR avoid direct conflict, fostering trust through scientific cooperation.

1963
12 minutes

After the Cuban Missile Crisis, the U.S. and USSR sign the Partial Test Ban Treaty, halting atmospheric nuclear tests and marking a step toward arms control.

1968
7 minutes

Amid Cold War tensions, wars erupt in Asia, nuclear powers expand, and regional conflicts risk escalating into global catastrophe, pushing the Doomsday Clock closer to midnight.

1969
10 minutes

The Nuclear Non-Proliferation Treaty unites most nations, limiting nuclear weapons while promoting peaceful nuclear power. Though some refuse to sign, it marks a step toward disarmament.

1972
12 minutes

The U.S. and USSR sign SALT and ABM treaties to limit missile launchers and defense systems, aiming for nuclear parity, though thousands of warheads remain.

1974
9 minutes

India tests its first nuclear bomb, while the U.S. and USSR modernize arsenals with MIRV technology, undermining arms control efforts.

1980
7 minutes

Amid stalled disarmament talks, global instability, and reckless nuclear policies, the world edges closer to disaster, pushing the Doomsday Clock forward.

1981
4 minutes

The Soviet invasion of Afghanistan and Reagan’s election harden U.S. nuclear policy, shifting from arms control to a more aggressive Cold War stance.

1984
3 minutes

U.S.-Soviet relations freeze, arms control talks stall, and the U.S. pushes for a space-based missile defense, risking a new arms race.

1988
6 minutes

In 1987, Reagan and Gorbachev sign the INF Treaty, banning an entire class of nuclear weapons, driven by European protests against escalating missile deployments.

1990
10 minutes

The fall of the Berlin Wall and Eastern European revolutions mark the Cold War’s end, reducing the nuclear threat and dismantling the myth of monolithic communism.

1991
17 minutes

With the Cold War over, the U.S. and Russia sign START, significantly cutting nuclear arsenals and reducing hair-trigger alert, debunking the illusion of nuclear security.

1995
14 minutes

A Russian misidentification of a US-Norwegian rocket nearly triggers nuclear war, fueling US fears of a resurgent Russia. With 40,000 nuclear weapons still in existence, concerns grow over nuclear security and terrorism.

1998
9 minutes

India and Pakistan conduct nuclear tests in 1998, exposing global failures in nuclear control, while Russia and the US keep thousands of warheads ready to launch.

2002
7 minutes

After 9/11, the US fears nuclear terrorism, seeks new nuclear weapons, rejects arms control treaties, and withdraws from the 1972 Anti-Ballistic Missile Treaty.

2007
5 minutes

North Korea tests a nuclear bomb, raising global concerns, while fears grow over Iran’s nuclear ambitions. The US and Russia remain on high alert.

2010
6 minutes

The Copenhagen climate summit sees a breakthrough as nations agree to limit global warming. Meanwhile, the US and Russia discuss further nuclear arms reductions, sparking hope for disarmament.

2012
5 minutes

Global challenges persist as nuclear disarmament, energy, and climate change remain unresolved. Rising tensions in Northeast Asia, the Middle East, and South Asia highlight nuclear risks, while safer reactors and technological solutions may not fully prevent climate-related crises.

2015
3 minutes

Global leaders fail to address nuclear threats and climate change, risking humanity’s future. The US and Russia modernize nuclear arsenals, undermining treaties, while climate efforts remain insufficient. The Doomsday Clock moves to three minutes to midnight.

2017
2.5 minutes

The Doomsday Clock moves closer to midnight, signaling growing global dangers. Experts urge leaders and citizens to act quickly to prevent catastrophe.

2018
2 minutes

The world faces imminent danger from nuclear war, climate change, and misinformation. Citizens must demand facts and action from leaders to ensure a safer future.

2020
100 seconds

The world is at its most dangerous point ever, with nuclear war, climate change, and cyber warfare threatening humanity. Leaders and citizens must act urgently to prevent catastrophe.

2023
90 seconds

The Doomsday Clock moves to 90 seconds to midnight, the closest ever to global catastrophe. The main reason is the war in Ukraine, which has destabilized international relations and increased global risks.

2025
89 seconds

the Doomsday Clock moves to 89 seconds to midnight, the closest ever. Despite clear dangers, global leaders have failed to act. The tiny shift emphasizes the extreme urgency of preventing catastrophe.

This timeline reproduces official Bulletin of the Atomic Scientists data. The Doomsday Clock® is a metaphor and does not predict actual time. Clock settings determined by the Bulletin's Science and Security Board.
